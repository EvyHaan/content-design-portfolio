WORK HELLO

Daily UX Writing Challenge

Daily UX Writing Challenge is 15 days of new UX Writing prompts to practice writing quick, helpful user-oriented copy. Find out more on the Daily UX Writing website.

Day 1


Scenario: A traveler is in an airport waiting for the last leg of a flight home when their flight gets abruptly canceled due to bad weather.


Challenge: Write a message from the airline app notifying them of the cancellation and what they need to do next.


Headline: 45 characters

Body: 175 characters max

Button(s): 25 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


In the headline, I wanted to be direct with the customer about 1) what had happened, and 2) why. Understanding why may not necessarily ease frustration. But given more context, a user can have a better sense of what to consider when figuring out what to do next.


There are three messages in the body. First, we empathize with the customer. They may be feeling annoyed, frustrated, or frantic. I want to acknowledge and validate those feelings.


Next, I tell the user what to do. I let them know they have two options. They can use the app to rebook their flight. Or, if they'd be more comfortable speaking with a person to navigate a stressful situation, we'd like to respect that preference.


Finally, I offer them something to look forward to.


The button gives them the opportunity to jump straight into rebooking, starting with viewing other flight options.

Day 2


Scenario: A user is a working parent, and a big sports fan, in the midst of their favorite sports season who can no longer attend games.


Challenge: Write a promotional screen for an app that lets a user choose teams, sends game reminders, real-time score updates and highlight videos.


Headline: 40 characters max

Body: 175 characters max

Button(s): 25 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


With the headline, I wanted to celebrate the user for balancing all they have going on in life while still being a devoted fan.


Because this is a peron without a lot of time, I am direct with the beneifts. I also assure them it's a low-effort relationship with the app. Set it and we'll never let you forget it (in a good way).


The CTA cuts right into the action to get them set up.

Day 3


Scenario: The user entered the wrong email address to sign in to their account.


Challenge: Tell the user to enter the right email.


40 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


Is it the wrong email? Or is the email wrong? We don't really know. All we know is that it doesn't match any email in our database.


So instead I tell the user what the problem is and what to do next. I keep it brief but conversational. The tone is direct and neutral, not negative or accusatory.

Day 4


Scenario: A user is in their favorite supermarket. They open the supermarket’s app on their phone to see what’s on sale and are greeted by a promotion.


Challenge: Write a promotional home screen for a subscription service that delivers groceries to the user once-a-month for a flat fee.


Headline: 45 characters max

Body: 175 characters max

Button(s): 25 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


A shopper may have any number of reasons why they might like outsourcing their grocery shopping. I didn't want to project generic speculative situations like "don't have time for shopping?" because they may or may not relate. So instead, in the headline, I just let them know grocery shopping is something we'd be excited to do for them, for whatever reason they may need it.


The body could have gone two directions: tell them more about the beneifts or address questions about how (not) complicated it is to use the service. I went with the later. I try to answer the immediate questions like "when would I get my groceries?" and "can I still use coupons?". Knowing how easy the set up is might sway someone to tap the sign up button right away.


Still, I offer the first button for this kind of scenario: the user opens the app while they are in the store. They're not going to sign up for a grocery shoppin service while they are already mid-shopping. But they may want to be reminded of it when the open the app at home.

Day 5


Scenario: The user works in graphic design. While critiquing a design in a mobile app, their phone abruptly turns off. When they restart the phone, they reopen the app.


Challenge: Write a message that the user will read immediately upon opening the app. What do they need to know? What steps (if any) do they need to take to recover their content? What if they can't recover the content?


Headline: 40 characters max

Body: 140 characters max

Button(s): 20 characters max


Solution:





Explanation:


In the first version, I address a situation where the user's work is permanantly lost. I wanted to be upfront with the user and let them know this fact without fluffing it up. This can be extremely frustrating. While there isn't anything that we can do to resolve the current issue, I felt it was important to assure them we do care and are concerned by the issue. It is important to let the know the problem is being worked on and their insight can help us prevent it from happening again. Because that's the action I'd most like them to take, "File A Report" is the primary action.


In the second version, I address a situation where their work was recovered. The tone is a little more relaxed. However, I still want to reassure them that we are addressing the issue, and any information they provide would be helpful. Their work is safe, but the whole circumstance is still an inconvenient interruption that is in everyone's interest to fix. In the primary action, they can get straight back to work, but we still offer the option to send us a report if they are willing.

Day 6


Scenario: It’s Monday. A user has just gotten into their car to drive to work. They plug their phone into the car and start driving.


Challenge: How would you let the user know there’s a fire happening in a nearby town that is causing road closures? The effect on their commute is unknown, but there is a definite danger if the fire gets closer. How do you communicate this to them? When? Write it.


Headline: 30 characters max

Body: 45 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


A push notification is the best way to relay an alert to a user as it does not require them to unlock their phone or open an app. They see (or hear or feel, depending on sound and haptic preferences) it right away. If the user is driving, they can only afford a glance, so the information must be succint and direct. This notification was designed with the idea that a user may tap it to open their navigation app which might suggest alternative routes with estimated commute times and/or visually display.


Day 7


Scenario: A sports fan is at a wedding while their favorite team is playing against their arch-rivals. Their team scores.


Challenge: How would you, quickly, let the sports fan know about the latest play, the current score, and the key players? Write it.


Headline: 30 characters max

Body: 45 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


For an at-a-glance update, a push notification is the best way to reach the user. I started with an exclamation "Goal!" so the user knows what has happened. Then I give the current score so they have context. This is one space I feel like using jargon can be appropriate - fans of the sport are likely already familiar enough with the specialized or slang terminology which helps describe the gist of the play.


Day 8


Scenario: The user is a casual music fan and (on occasion) goes to live concerts. They have a music player app on their phone.


Challenge: Tell the user that one of their favorite bands is playing live in their town. How would you compel them to want to go?


Headline: 30 characters max

Body: 45 characters max

Button: 25 characters


Solution:



Explanation:


For this challenge, I modeled my design from Apple Music which is my personal go-to music app. I considered the components in place already from what I could gather of the design system for the app.


The most important things to user to help them decide if they would like to go is the band name, the date and time, and the location. While I considered adding catchy body text to include either a recognizable lyric or ideas that embody the band (emerald jumpers and keytars), I wanted to keep the replicability of the design easy, including the formatting and pattern of the text.


The date is somewhat reiterated in the title, but in an easier way for the user to process as upcoming soon.


While the playlist, song, artist, and special features cards throughout the rest of the app ARE buttons, I chose to use a link below the card for "Get Tickets". This was to avoid the user having problems accidentally tapping into the order tickets screen while scrolling or fumbling with phone and keys and whatever else in hand because of the large tappable area. Using the link below minimizes the risk, helps it stand out, and may serve as a signal that it may link to a different app or website.

Day 10


Scenario: The user is trying to view a website to help them buy a car. But, the content can’t load without the user’s location. They need to enter their ZIP code and first name.


Challenge: Ask them where they live and who they are without sounding like you're unnecessarily mining their data.


Headline: 25 characters max

Body: 45 characters max

Button: 15 characters


Solution:



Explanation:


While it's clear to me why the user's location is valuable here, it's not so clear why I need their first name. What I don't know I can't convey to the user. On a real project, I would take this question back to the PM.


In the meantime, I supposed this information may be used to begin a user account. The body conveys to the user that once provided, they can then begin favoriting cars in their area they'd like to consider. The language is simple and honest in what the information will be used for.


Prior to asking for the user's first name, however, the title conveys a tone of familiarity and camraderie by using the first person plural language. A user might be excited by the prospect or dread the process getting a car, and the voice of the website is open to support the customer through the process.


Day 11


Scenario: An elderly user is doing a Google search to find an easy way to buy contact lenses online.


Challenge: Write a title and meta description for a website that sells subscription contact lenses delivered to a user every 30 days—convince them to try it.


Title: 60 characters max

160 characters: 45 characters max


Solution:



Explanation:


I want to give the user as much information as possible, in a seemingly impossibly small space. An elderly user might be concerned about price, hidden costs, if their insurance or medicare is accepted, getting trapped in bad commitment, and whether they can find the brand and type they are familiar with using.


First, I try to draw in their attention to the benefit of a monthly subscription. They will have a supply ready to go for the whole month, worry-free and hassle-free. The company name is also in the title for SEO searchability.


Next I try to address as many of their questions as possible. While keeping in mind this search is specifically by an elderly user, it could just as well be a search by any other demographic. This means I still want to keep the information relatively general. An example of where I broaden the language is to include "We bill your insurance directly. The goal here is to indicate the company will interface directly with insurers in general. It's an invitation for the user to look into the site deeper or talk to a representative about more specific insurance questions.
