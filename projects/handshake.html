Handshake App

Project Type

Practice design challenge


Duration

Jan 2022, 3 weeks


Role

UX writer


Tools

Overview

Handshake is a (fictional) new project management and payments app for freelancers and business owners. While still in early design iterations, a main priority is to develop and document a content strategy from the outset.


The designer for the Handshake App provided mock-ups for an initial review with the Head of UX and VP of Product. To help the designer prepare for the design review, I was tasked with updating the text to build a better relationship with users. This included developing the voice & tone for Handshake, creating a style guide, and editing the copy.


After conducting a competitor analysis to better understand the industry and the app’s niche, I crafted a persona that would “be” Handshake. These helped define guiding principles and guide content revisions.


The outcome: a personable, yet professional voice that is embodied in the new copy and documented for continued development.

Challenge

Handshake supports the working relationship between freelancers and business owners. Although both parties use the same app, they have different needs. Freelancers are the primary audience, using the app for billing and progress reporting. Business owners use the app to track progress and make payments. Within the app, both types of user can define project features, negotiate budget and timeline, and send messages.


The challenge is to design UX copy that is consistent across both user flows while still being flexible in supporting the unique tasks of both types of users.


Additionally, with the upcoming high-level design review, I needed to avoid making significant flow and interraction changes. Those would come in later iterations.

Solution


DISCOVERY


Design documents review

The designer provided a target audience personas, an overview of the app goals and features, and a first-iteration mockup. I started by reviewing the personas to get into the mindset of the end-user.

With the personas in mind, I walked through the screens to determine the goal for each. I wrote all questions and thoughts that came to mind about UI elements, interaction flows, and the clarity of the information presented.


Competitor analysis

I was unfamiliar with the freelancing industry. I chose to conduct a competitive analysis, to find out:


1. the language used in the industry by competitor apps and end-users

2. common formatting or styling challenges to anticipate

3. if/how each competitor app conveyed the personality of their app

4. how the end-users connected with that personality


I searched for competitor apps highlighted in freelancer resource blogs. Three apps stood out as direct competitors based on audience, primary function, features, and popularity. These were Honeybook, Fiverr Workspace, and Upwork.


First, I stepped through any freely accessible onboarding steps or features to get a feel for the app’s personality and piece together a rationale behind their content design decisions.


Next, I scanned reviews of the app over the previous year to understand how users described themselves, their collaboration partners, and their work. I also noted points of friction or delight related to specific copy or personality traits.


A major finding was that freelancers often described themselves as running their own business. This blurring of the line between Handshake’s audiences simplified the process for developing character traits for the app.


Another finding was that only one competitor had an obvious, well-defined product persona. Its consistent presence was effective. This meant that defining a clear voice would be crucial for Handshake.



IDEATION


Voice

I created “Casey”, a product persona to embody the Handshake app. If the user was their own CEO, Casey was to be their executive assistant. Casey would provide only the right information in the right amount at the right time, while also being available for extra support as needed. Casey is someone who is organized and transparent while being approachable with a bright positivity.

Fig 3. Slide deck introducing Casey, the voice & tone of Handshake

Tone

I defined two tonal ranges to show how Casey might adapt to user needs. The first range was supporting-to-guiding, and the second from playful-to-serious. Intersecting these ranges, I charted 13 interface touchpoints a user would experience.

The majority of touchpoints lean toward being supporting, giving the user the most control, and only taking the lead when asked or needed. The touchpoints were equal in the playful-to-serious range. Most interactions that could be playful were either short-lived or infrequent, i.e. feature tours or empty states. Conversely, informational copy needed to be ambient, while help or warning copy needed to be direct.


Style guide

Getting Handshake’s personality right was important. Conveying it consistently would make or break the relationship with the user. Consistency builds trust and confidence.


Considering the early stage of the product, I considered long-term impacts. Two questions guided my decisions:


1. How can I develop guidelines that both enforce consistency in this early iteration while leaving room for Handshake to mature over time?

2. What can I plan for now that will ease future style and formatting challenges?


To answer the first question, the governing rules for component styles and copy formatting stayed fairly banal. They are concise without being restrictive. I included examples for easy reference.

For the second question, I kept in mind potential new features, regional markets, translation and localization, inclusivity, and accessibility. I allowed these to guide current decisions for the present presentation of information.

DESIGN


Mockup revisions

It was finally time to revise the text. I did this in three stages:


1. A first pass to speed through and change all the text. This rephrasing exercise helped me grasp what the designer intended to convey by each section of text.

2. A second pass to revise the text and copy components within the voice and style guidelines.

3. A third pass to make any final adjustments, revisit the intended meaning for each screen, and review for a cohesive personality presence across the app.

Fig 7. The welcome screen was a fairly blank slate from design. I added the company name for brand recognition, a more inspiring title, and product benefits the user could look forward to.

Fig 8. I separated the sign in and sign up flows into different screens for clarity. I chose "Create an account" over "Sign in" to reduce cognitive load by the added visual distinction. Additionally, I added redirecting links in case the user needed to switch flow tracks. The microcopy for the password was simplified. Input labels & hints as well as a CTA were added to each flow.

Fig 9. Where the designer saw this as the "next" screen to signing up for the app, this really served as the start of several onboarding screens. Subsequent screens are oriented around the selected role, although still parallel in flow. I also softened the title to be more inquisitive rather than interrogative, as to more closely reflect the tone of Handshake. The instruction text did not directly relate to the immediate question so I removed it.
The terms "freelancer" and "project owner" were chosen based on findings from the competitive analysis. The primary audience strongly identified themselves as "freelancers", but also as business owners in their own right. I maintained "freelancer" for familiarity. The frequently stated alternative for "business owner" was "client". However, the Handhsake team felt it set a mood of imbalance, rather than one of collaboration. Additionally, freelancers may have thier own projects they hire out for. With this in mind, I pitched "project owner" for future discussion by the team.

Fig 10. First, I made the title parallel for both types of users. Financials may be an uncomfortable topic, so Handshakes introduces it in a confident and matter-of-fact yet conversational way. Additional microcopy tells the user that their payment details will be expected next, so they can get those ready. Asterisks on each option signaled the designer should include a tooltip. Here, the tooltips would let the user know processing times for each payment method. It was also important to call out legal terms of service agreements. I added copy on the screen for the relevant user, but requested the Handshake team set a meeting with the in-house legal team to determine appropriate terms of service communication.

Fig 11. The project dashboard is parallel for each user, but contains slightly different information based on the user's role. I re-labeled this section "invoices" for continuity between the two views. Concise but descriptive section headers and field labels improved the scannability. Date and cost formats were also revised for improved readability. The CTA buttons were revised according to the styleguide.
It is also important to call out here that the information being presented is unclear. I requested a meeting with the designer and project manager to clarify the heirarchy around how to present the invoices in different states, including created, sent, awaiting payment, and paid.

Fig 12. After a user sends an invoice, they have the ability to cancel it. The dialog was confusing. I added a clear title, with a CTA that clearly stated the action and matched the language of the title. The description was simplified to clearly state what would happen.

Fig 13. The project messaging center is another parallel screen in both user flows. My main suggestion to the designer was that we revamp this screen completely in a future iteration, but I wanted to first understand thier rationale for the current designs. There are established emailing and messages conventions that we could safely assume are familiar to the user and we can take advantage of.
To support the present designs, I removed the project title since this is a screen already living within the specified project dashboard. I clarified the section headings and made them consistent across both flows, as well as added empty state copy.

Fig 14. The provided designs did not include a screen displaying all the projects a user might have in progress. I pitched a mock-up of an idea for this screen. By greeting the user by name, it adds a layer of familiarity to the user's relationship with Handshake. On this screen the user is able to view key details of a project at a glance, sort projects, and complete quick actions.

Fig 15. I proposed a redesign of the project dashboard to support continuity across flows and address navigation issues in the original design. This included adding the project title above the pagination tabs so the user always knows what project they are working in. The tab labels serve as proxies for screen titles. Above the title, are navigation, the account menu, and project-wide actions. Below the tabs, many of the original design layouts are maintained.

WRAP-UP


Managing team relationships

Where interactions and flows were not clear, I asked for clarity from the designer or product manager. I proposed solutions to friction points or anticipated feature integration issues. I also highlighted where we may need to cross-collaborate with other teams, bringing in spokespeople from legal, marketing, engineering, localization, and research. Their expertise would be critical to inform the decisions and forecast feasibility, especially at this early stage.


Testing & Analytics

While I did not create a full testing strategy, I came across a few elements of copy that gave me pause about the best text to choose. I made decisions based on quick word usage via Google Analytics, but noted alternate options that may be worth A/B testing.


Documentation

Project Board

To track project progress, I created a project board on Trello. Breaking down the entire project into manageable tasks helped keep the project moving, and gave me a space to organize to-dos and celebrate milestones.

Organization

Throughout the process, I maintained a Google Drive directory that included all the files relevant to the project. This provided visibility to all documents, processes, and decisions. With this, any team member could feel confident about contributing to the project.


Versioning

As I revised copy, some decisions I had made in the style guide were no longer applicable. I didn’t want to simply change the style guide. I wanted to document the changes and provide a rationale to serve as a reference. I introduced document versioning to govern protocols for making changes ranging from minor typo fixes, to large revisions requiring new releases.

Reflection

This was the final project for the Fundamentals of UX Writing course offered by the UX Content Collective and my first real sleeves-rolled-up plunge into the daily work of a UX writer. The aim was to update the copy to better connect with and serve the user, without changing interactions or flows.


What I found most challenging was working within concept designs that were incomplete. Oftentimes the interactions and flows had glaring flaws. This was a great exercise in team collaboration. It made me consider how I would want to communicate with my team about product decisions. It also made me realize how important it was to articulate my own design decisions.


What surprised me was seeing the impact of content-early design at the start of a product. The mock-ups already existed. But several times I wanted to change the entire layout to make the copy, sections, and screen concepts more consistant and clear across flows. It made me appreciate the importance of being involved in user research processes, defining the information hierarchy, and developing a content strategy as early as possible.
