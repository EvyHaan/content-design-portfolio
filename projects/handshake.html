WORK HELLO

Handshake App

This is my final project for the UX Writing Fundamentals course from UX Content Collective where I collaborate with a fictional team on a fictional product.

Project Type

Practice design challenge


Duration

Jan - Feb 2022


Role

UX writer


Tools

Figma, Trello, Google Docs/Sheets/Slides, diagrams.net

Overview


Handshake is a (fictional) new project management and payments app for freelancers and business owners. While still in early design iterations, a main priority is to develop and document a content strategy from the outset.


I was tasked with the following deliverables:

  • A style guide
  • Revise UX copy

I addressed the immediate task in 4 phases. To set the project up for success, I planned for a collaboration phase as a next step.

  • Understand
  • Research
  • Ideate
  • Revise
  • Collaborate

Considering the upcoming design review, I needed to accomplish these with minimal changes to the interactions and flows. Any flaws would be addressed in future iterations.


The outcome was a personable, yet professional voice that is embodied by new UX copy on the Handshake app and well-documented for continued development.

Phase 1: Understand


The Handshake app supports collaboration between freelancers and business owners. Both groups use the same app but experience different flows targeted to their needs.


Actions for freelancers:

  • Set a pay rate
  • Propose a budget
  • Track hours
  • Send invoices
  • Communicate about the project

Actions for business owners:

  • Define project details & features
  • Approve a budget proposal
  • Review time reports
  • View invoices
  • Send payments
  • Communicate about the project

Both parties may bring very different experiences and perspectives to the app. Other questions I considered:

  • Do they have experience with this type of working relationship? How much?
  • How experienced are they in their own position?
  • Are they technically savvy?
  • Have they tried other similar apps? Was it a positive or negative experience?
  • What is the physical setting where they may use the app?

With the users in mind, I walked through the initial design mockups. I noted the objective of each section and the main goal each page conveyed. These sections were:

  • Welcome
  • Onboarding - freelancer
  • Onboarding - business owner
  • Project dashboard - freelancer
    • Project details
    • Time reporting
    • Invoicing center
  • Project dashboard - business owner
    • Project details
    • Time reviewing
    • Payments center
  • Messaging center - freelancer
  • Messaging center - business owner

Phase 2: Research


Competitive Analysis: Honeybook, Fiverr, & Upwork


I conducted a competitive analysis to find out:

  1. The language used in the industry by competitor apps and end-users
  2. Common formatting or styling challenges to anticipate
  3. If/how the personality of each app was conveyed by the competitor
  4. How the end-users connected with that personality

Honeybook, Fiverr Workspace, and Upwork stood out as the top competitors based on audience, primary function, features, and popularity.


Analysis of each app happened in two parts:


First, I stepped through any freely accessible onboarding and features screens. This gave me a feel for each app’s content design decisions, especially around formatting dates, numbers, costs, phrase structuring, and sentence casing. I also got a feel for the language used to connect with the user.


Second, I scanned reviews of the app. I looked for reviews where users described themselves, their collaboration partnership, and their work to understand the terminology of the freelance industry. I also looked for points of friction or delight related to specific copy or “personality traits” of the app.


Of all the findings, two stood out:

  1. Freelancers often described themselves as running their own business. This blurred the distinction between Handshake’s two audiences. I would need to redefine the roles for clarity.
  2. Only one competitor had a well-defined persona. Its consistent presence was highly effective. Defining a clear voice and tone would be crucial for Handshake to build a relationship with its users.

Phase 3: Ideate


Meet Casey: the voice & tone of Handshake


If the user was their own CEO, they needed an executive assistant. That was the inspiring idea for “Casey”, who would be the spokesperson - the voice - Handshake.


Casey would provide the right information in the right amount at the right time. They provide ambient support, preemptively giving key information without being obtrusive. Casey is someone who is organized and transparent while being approachable with a bright positivity. These traits inspired the guiding principles and became the voice.


Casey’s tone adapts to the user’s needs on two ranges: Supporting-to-Guiding and Serious-to-Playful.


Intersecting these ranges, I charted 13 interface touch points across a tonal schematic.


On the Supporting-to-Guiding range, most points leaned toward being supporting. This gives the user the most autonomy with the goal of seamlessly integrating into their daily workflow. Casey only takes the lead when asked or to navigate precarious situations.


On the Serious-to-Playful range, the touchpoints were fairly balanced. Worth noting, most “playful” interactions are infrequent or short-lived. Examples are feature tours in onboarding, or empty states. Conversely, copy that was viewed regularly, like instruction text, or copy that needed careful review, like warnings, needed to offer transparency and a direct way forward.



Style Guide: a blueprint for consistency


Consistency builds trust and confidence. These ideas are at the heart of the app.


Considering the early state of the product, I considered long-term impacts. Two questions guided my decisions:

  1. How can I develop guidelines that both enforce consistency in this early iteration while leaving room for Handshake to mature over time?
  2. What can I anticipate now that will alleviate future style and formatting challenges?

The governing rules for component styles and copy formatting were designed to be basic. I included examples for easy reference.


I kept in mind potential new features, regional markets, translation and localization, inclusivity, and accessibility. These ideas guided decisions for the present presentation of information.

Phase 4: Revise


Revisions happened in three stages:

  1. A first fast-pass to change all the text. This quick rephrasing exercise helped me grasp what the designer intended each section of copy to convey. I noted anything that was unclear or felt unorganized.
  2. A second pass to closer align the copy and components to the style guidelines.
  3. A third pass to make final adjustments, revisit the intended goal for each screen, and review for a cohesive personality presence across the app.

The following are some highlights.

Phase 5: Collaborate on next steps


Where interactions, flows, or actions were not clear, I posed questions for the designer or product manager to understand the mechanics or goals. I proposed solutions to friction points or anticipated feature integration issues. I also highlighted where we may need to cross-collaborate with other teams, bringing in spokespeople from legal, marketing, engineering, localization, and research. Their expertise would be critical to inform the decisions and forecast feasibility.


One goal for this project was to make the progress trackable and easy to contribute to. I created a project board. Tickets had well-defined acceptance criteria and story points. I organized all project files in a Google Drive directory. Additionally, I implemented versioning for the style guide to track design decisions overtime.

Reflection

At first, I found it challenging to integrate my objectives as the team’s UX Writer into designs that were already mocked up. I had to shift my mindset into a real-life setting of collaboration and iteration. With that lens I was able to move forward.


Without the constraints of the project, some things I would do differently or add:

  • Map the information architecture (IA)
  • Redesign screens based on the new IA
  • Expand the style guide into a larger content design system
  • Develop a testing strategy
  • And of course, have continuous conversations with team members for the best forward momentum
